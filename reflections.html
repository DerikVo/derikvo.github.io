My thoughts on teaching, learning, and equity.

Reflection: Teacher Biases, Empathy, and Cultural Awareness

Date: August 2, 2025

This week, I learned about how different biases can impact teachers. The "curse of knowledge" really stood out. This is when a teacher assumes students have the background knowledge to understand a topic, which can highlight an inability to understand a student's zone of proximal development. This bias can also lead to a misalignment between a teacher's and a student's learning styles. As we learned in class, when students are unfamiliar with "rote" learning, they spend more time building those skills. Because of that, they may fall behind and be viewed from a deficit perspective rather than from their strengths. As a result, these students are often mislabeled as low performing.

Additionally, I've witnessed how survivorship bias and confirmation bias can impact a student. I've seen teachers use success stories of similar students to overlook a student's struggles. I've seen teachers say a student didn't need accommodations because other students like them didn't. For example, there was a time a teacher denied an accommodation for a student experiencing bereavement. This teacher didn't budge until higher-ups were involved. These biases and lack of empathy have taught me the importance of building empathy and perspective-taking in teachers. Every student has different needs and levels of resilience, and teachers need to be more understanding of the unique support each student requires.

Other than that, I enjoyed learning about other people's cultures during the "Who am I?" presentations. Particularly how anti-caste system Indians use their father's and/or mother's name for their last name. So some may feel weird being called by their last name because it is their parent's name.

Reflection: The Nuances of ADHD Diagnosis and Intervention

Date: July 25, 2025

One thing I learned this week was about the prevalence of ADHD diagnoses in eastern states. It calls into question the granularity of what populations are being diagnosed and medicated for ADHD. It reminds me of a therapist I've talked to who mentions people of color are often misdiagnosed because cultural experiences are not accounted for. For example, they mention how African-American male teens are more likely to be diagnosed with schizophrenia even though they clearly display signs of severe depression (Gara et al., 2023).

Do these students have ADHD, or are they being misdiagnosed? It reminds me of students I've worked with in special ed that get put on medication due to behavior problems who lose all their personality and become very compliant, which was brought up in the video we watched in class. Are we supporting students with ADHD? Or are we using ADHD as an excuse to force students to become compliant? I bring this up because I was on the opposite end of this dilemma; I had undiagnosed ADHD and instead of receiving support for it, I was labeled as an English language learner even though I was only fluent in English. Although I was able to sit still, I had difficulty focusing on relevant tasks and struggled with prioritizing them. So it comes into question: how many students are misdiagnosed with ADHD, and how many are undiagnosed?

Another point that I found interesting during our discussion is whether medication is age-appropriate. Are we expecting preschool children to sit for long periods of time and labeling them as misbehaving if they don't? Circling back to misdiagnosis, are we pushing for an ADHD diagnosis because we believe not being able to sit still is a symptom? It reminds me of the pyramid of intervention. Why do people often jump to medication instead of accommodating the environment or material? It should go from the least invasive to the most intensive intervention. I hope that I can become more aware of any biases and assumptions I have to prevent perpetuating inequity, such as the teacher mentioned in the reading that believed families living in poverty were not investing in education. I hope I have a trusted network of colleagues that will challenge any biases I may hold.

Reflection: TPEs, Adaptive Technology, and Professional Growth

Date: July 19, 2025

One of the things I learned this week is the "teacher performance expectations" (TPEs) and the competencies that are expected of incoming teachers. Specifically, sections 1.4 and 4.4 mention a multi-tiered system of supports, which is something I referenced in a discussion post. One thing I'm excited about that was mentioned in the TPEs is the use of adaptive technology. Through experience working with medically fragile students who require various adaptations involving switches, I often find myself (as a paraeducator) collaborating with teachers, occupational therapists, physical therapists, and adapted P.E. teachers to adapt activities to allow students to participate. For example, using switches and devices to pour dirt and seeds, turn on watering hoses, and communicate in a gardening activity. Then as a part of earth science, we categorize things we grow as well as smell the different fragrances of the herbs we grow.

By reviewing the TPEs, I was able to identify what I have done and areas where I need to develop. Although the document is dense, I enjoy skimming through to find areas of strength/experience or areas of deficit/growth. For example, in area 4.5, I have experience in writing (mock) IEP and IFSP forms, but it has been years since I've done them. Additionally, I don't have experience with ITP or 504 plans, so that helped me identify my zone of proximal growth. This document can be paired with mentor feedback and self-reflection to better align my competencies to teacher performance expectations.
